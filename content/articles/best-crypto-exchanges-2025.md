Not all crypto exchanges are created equal, and the one you start with shapes your entire crypto experience: fee structures determine how much of your investment you keep, security track records determine whether you keep it at all, and available assets determine what strategies are accessible to you.

This guide focuses on five key criteria that actually matter for most buyers: security track record, fee structures, supported assets, ease of use, and geographic availability. We've deliberately avoided recommending exchanges with history of security incidents, regulatory evasion, or deceptive practices.

<Callout type="warning">
**Critical reminder:** Exchanges are not wallets. Funds on an exchange are custodied by that company. For any amount you're not actively trading, transfer to a hardware wallet. "Not your keys, not your coins" is not a cliché — it's the lesson from FTX, Celsius, BlockFi, and Mt. Gox.
</Callout>

## Exchange Security Checklist

Before depositing funds on any exchange, verify:

- ✅ Is the exchange regulated in your jurisdiction?
- ✅ Does it have a publicly verifiable security audit history?
- ✅ Does it offer proof of reserves verification?
- ✅ Is two-factor authentication (preferably hardware key or authenticator app — not SMS) available?
- ✅ What is their history with security incidents and how did they respond?

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Should I keep crypto on an exchange or in a hardware wallet?**
For any amount beyond what you're actively trading, use a hardware wallet. Exchanges are custodians — if they're hacked, frozen by regulators, or go bankrupt, your funds are at risk. See our [hardware wallet guide](/articles/best-hardware-wallets-2025) for recommendations.

**Are crypto exchange fees tax-deductible?**
In most jurisdictions, trading fees reduce your cost basis (and thus your taxable gain) on crypto transactions. Consult a tax professional familiar with crypto in your jurisdiction.

**Is Binance safe to use in 2025?**
Binance.US (the US entity) is available in most US states, though its regulatory situation has been complex. The global Binance entity reached a $4.3B settlement with the US DOJ in 2023 but continues operating. Risk-tolerant traders use it for low fees; risk-averse buyers prefer Coinbase, Kraken, or Gemini.

**What's the difference between a centralized and decentralized exchange?**
Centralized exchanges (Coinbase, Kraken, Binance) custody your assets and are regulated entities. Decentralized exchanges (Uniswap, dYdX) use smart contracts — you retain custody of your assets but take on smart contract risk. DEXs are better for privacy and DeFi participation; CEXs are better for fiat on/off ramping and regulatory safety.
