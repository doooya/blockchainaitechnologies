Today, it means subject tracking systems that lock onto a human eye and follow it seamlessly across a frame, deep learning models that optimize exposure in real time, and computational photography pipelines that process thousands of images per second.

This guide cuts through the feature noise to focus on AI capabilities that measurably improve photos and video — particularly for content creators, portrait photographers, wildlife shooters, and professionals who need their gear to reliably keep up with their subjects.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do AI camera features work in manual mode?**
Most AI autofocus features (subject tracking, eye detection) work independently of exposure mode — you can use them with manual exposure settings. AI scene recognition for automatic exposure typically only activates in auto or semi-auto modes.

**Is AI autofocus better than manual focus for video?**
For run-and-gun video and documentary-style shooting, yes significantly. For fully controlled narrative video with predictable subject positions, experienced camera operators can still match or exceed AI tracking. For most content creators, AI tracking is the better default.

**What's the best AI camera under $1,000?**
The Sony ZV-E10 II inherits Sony's AI tracking in a compact body. For video-focused work under $1,000, the GoPro Hero 12 + DJI Mini 4 Pro drone combination gives more creative flexibility than a single mirrorless camera.

**Do more megapixels improve AI autofocus?**
Resolution and autofocus are separate systems. The Canon R6 II at 20MP has more reliable tracking than many higher-resolution cameras. Autofocus performance depends on the on-chip phase detection pixel density and the AI model, not the total resolution.
